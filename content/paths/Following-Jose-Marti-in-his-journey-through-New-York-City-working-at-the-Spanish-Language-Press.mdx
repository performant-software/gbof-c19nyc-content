    blurb: >
      Description:


      This address was Jose Martí's first stop in New York from Cuba on his
      journey to working at the Spanish-Language Press. He lived here for a
      short period of time, while he adjusted to his new life. (1880-1880).


      Historical Context:


      This boarding house Martí stayed at was operated by Carmen Mantilla, from
      whom he later had personal relations with. This boarding house was a hub
      for Cuban exiles "known to the revolutionists and independistas" as well
      as known friends of Mantilla.


      Significance:


      This boarding house started Martí's career in New York and may have even
      given him the push to work on the Spanish-Language press, amongst other
      Cuban writers and artist.

    blurb: >
      Description:


      Jose Martí lived at this boarding house in 1881, this became the second
      location he lived at during his time in New York. At this boarding house,
      Martí began to start sending articles to the newspaper “La Opinión
      Nacional of Caracas”, staring his career in NYC.


      Historical Context:


      In this newspaper, Martí published a piece of writing named Letter from
      New York, from which he left his signature anonymous as “M. de: Z.”, until
      four months later. He did however, leave the words "from our
      correspondent" under the letter he wrote, which indicated that the writer
      resided in New York. There are rumors as to why he did this, some suggest
      Martí was worried about displeasing the Venezuelan president Antonio
      Guzmán Blanco.


      Significance:


      This site is significant because it showcases the start of Jose Martí's
      career in the Spanish-Language press.

      Description:


      This boarding house became Martí's third stop throughout the multiple
      boarding houses he utilized while in New York. At this boarding house, he
      published Ismaelillo in 1882.


      Historical Context:


      This publication is a book made up of fifteen poems, dedicated to his son
      José Francisco Martí. Within these poems, Martí expresses his great
      nostalgia for his homeland (Havana, Cuba), his son and childhood as a
      "golden age". Jose Martí was imprisoned as a teenager while in Cuba, which
      is why he turns to adolescence/childhood nostalgia to find a sense of
      comfort.


      Significance:


      This site helps put into perspective the effort and energy Jose Martí put
      into his work while contributing to the Spanish-Language press.

      Description:


      This boarding house became Jose Martí's fourth address in New York in the
      year 1883. Here, he Published La América (America).


      Historical Context:


      In this publication, Martí wrote about the amount of immigrants that had
      arrived in New York during the month of May that year. Martí specified
      different nations that had arrived and the attributes each one brought. As
      the University of Texas Rio Grande Valley wrote, he explained each nation
      as " France is “fierce” \[fiera] (VIII, 378), and Sweden is “poor and
      cold, as well as beautiful and original” \[pobre y fría, tanto como bella
      y original] (VIII, 378)". In his writing, he expressed concern that the
      foreigners who had traveled to the United States would influence the
      democracy that was held, which he considered to be a model for Latin
      America


      Significance:


      This publication is significant because it shows the contrast in content
      between his previous writings, further attributing to his role involved in
      the Spanish-Language press.
