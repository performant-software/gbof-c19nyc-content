      **Description:**


      This location, at 40 and 42 Broadway, specialized in Spanish and Latin
      American books. Additionally, this location housed the printing offices of
      Nestor Ponce de León - where the Cuban author and activist laid the
      groundwork for Spanish Printing Presses in New York


      **Historical Importance, Context:**


      This is the third and final location of Nestor Poncé de León’s bookstore
      and was known to be crucial for the distribution of Spanish-Language
      literature. In a time period with limited Spanish-langauge bookstores and
      publishers, León's establishment(s) allowed for the circulation of many
      genres: fiction, non fiction, political, and more.


      **Significance to Pathway and Works Cited:**


      The final site in this pathway wraps up the steady relocating of Nestor
      Poncé de León’s publishing offices. Understanding the growth and movement
      of publishers and writers is essential when analysing how Spanish-language
      literature expanded across New York City. Having not one, not two, but
      three separate locations shows the dedication and tenacity that describes
      19th century Latin activists and their efforts towards inclusivity and
      solidarity.

This pathway explores the connection between a series of bookstores owned by Nestor Ponce de León in connection to both a political and cultural organization of the time. The sites connect to one another through stories of solidarity in 19th century New York City, with a special emphasis on understanding how these community bookstores and publishing offices expanded literature beyond the readers hands, and allowed marginalized communities (in our case Latin American authors and intellectuals in NYC) to form groups that battled inequality in America and overseas. While looking through this pathway, it is encouraged to trace the movement of León's establishments and their correlation with political and cultural awareness movements of the time. Viewers will have the opportunity to explore the deep connection between literary works and the formation of an independent Latin American identity in NYC. Furthermore, this topic allows for Latin American history in NYC to go beyond surface level and dive into the complicated history of Anti-Slavery committees, cultural and intellectual societies for Cuban revolutionaries, and even connects to present day communities. There are multiple resources for viewers to do personal research alongside the guided site information, including the limitations within this pathway.
