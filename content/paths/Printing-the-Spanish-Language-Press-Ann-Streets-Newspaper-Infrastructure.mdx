      ## **William Newell Printing House**


      ##### **Description**


      William Newell Printing House operated on Nassau Street, near Ann St.,
      printing a variety of publications in nineteenth-century New York.
      According to Lisandro Pérez, this print shop produced the first volume of
      Félix Varela's Cartas al Elpidio (Letters to Elpidio) in 1835.


      ##### **Historical Context** 


      This site provides a reminder that Spanish-language publishing in New York
      City reached back to the early nineteenth century. It also demonstrates
      that many writers and editors relied on commercial printers whose
      specialties were not limited to Spanish-language publishing. It was not
      until the later nineteenth-century that Spanish-language publishers and
      editors began to establish their own printing presses.


      ##### **Significance**


      ##### This site helps illustrate how Spanish-language publications
      depended on the broader printing economy of Lower Manhattan, especially as
      the community was getting its start earlier in the nineteenth century.


      ##### **Work Cited**


      Lisandro Pérez, Sugar, Cigars, and Revolution: The Making of Cuban New
      York, New York University Press, 2018.

      ## Ricardo de Requesens Print Shop


      ##### **Description**


      Among the clients of the Ricardo de Requesens print shop was \*La Revista
      Mercantil de Nueva York \*(New York Mercantile Magazine), which used the
      print shop's services in 1886, before the publication set up its own print
      shop. This paper later became \*La Revista Ilustrada de Nueva York,
      \*under the editorship of Nicanor Bolet Peraza.


      ##### **Historical Context**


      Spanish-language publications in New York often relied on networks of
      printers located close to newspaper offices. These relationships made it
      easier to produce publications quickly and distribute them throughout the
      city.


      ##### **Significance**


      The presence of multiple printers--who printed in multiple languages--on
      the same street suggests that this area functioned as a center of printing
      activity for New York City's multilingual print culture in the nineteenth
      century. It also likely meant that Ann St. was a place where newspaper
      editors crossed paths as they went about their work.

      ## **Imprenta de La Revista Ilustrada de Nueva York (New York Illustrated
      Magazine Print Shop)**


      ##### **Description**


      This was the site of the print shop of \*La Revista Ilustrada de Nueva
      York \*before it moved to the publication's offices on Reade St.


      ##### **Historical Context**


      We can see in the movement of sites where \*La Revista Ilustrada de Nueva
      York \*was printed a glimpse of the business model of the
      nineteenth-century Spanish-language press. Small publications started by
      hiring print shops, but they often purchased their own printing press once
      they had demonstrated some success. This enabled them to add job printing
      (the printing of flyers, business cards, and other business-related print
      needs) as a way make money along with advertising and subscriptions.


      ##### **Significance**


      This advertisement for the publication's new print shop shows this
      publication's development since it changed its name from \*La Revista
      Mercantil de Nueva York, \*under the editorship of Nicanor Bolet Peraza.

      ## Hoe and Company Showroom


      ##### **Description**


      Printers could purchase the latest models of Hoe and Company presses at
      this showroom.


      Historical Context: New York City was a leading site of print technology
      innovation in the nineteenth century. Hoe & Company was a leader in
      printing press innovation during that period.


      ##### **Significance**


      This showroom was likely another spot visited by editors and writers of
      the Spanish-language press in New York City. The company advertised in
      Spanish-language newspapers, and its catalog also indicates that presses
      could be designed in parts to be assembled later: "Presses of large size
      destined for Mexico, South America, or other mountainous regions where
      there are no carriage roads, have their frames made so that they can be
      taken to pieces and packed in smaller packages" (62).

      ## **Imprenta America Print Shop**


      ##### **Description**


      Founded by Sotero Figueroa after his arrival in New York City from San
      Juan Puerto Rico in 1889, Imprenta América served New York City's
      Spanish-language publications


      ##### **Historical Context**


      Imprenta América published important Spanish-language newspapers that
      promoted Cuban and Puerto Rican indepedence from Spain, including \*Patria
      \*and *El Porvenir.*


      ##### **Significance**


      Sotera Figueroa was an active printer, editor, and organizer for Cuban and
      Puerto Rican independence. His print shop also served as an anchor of the
      Spanish-language publishing community in the late nineteenth century in
      New York City. While many publications, such as \*La Revista Ilustrada de
      Nueva York \*had their own print shops and printed small jobs in addition
      to their own publications, Imprenta América distinguished itself as a
      print shop first (not a publication) that specialized exclusively in
      Spanish-language publishing.


      **Limits of this Virtual Walk**


      The sites on this virtual walk provide a glimpse of the print shops that
      produced Spanish-language publications in nineteenth-century New York
      City. One of the challenges of recovering this history is that, unlike
      some of the major publishing houses of the English-language press of the
      period, none of these small print shops left records that remain
      accessible to researchers today. Their advertisements and
      locations--especially the frequent shifts and relocations that we see in
      print shops and the Spanish-language publications that use them--provide a
      sense of the ways in which Spanish-language publications made use of small
      print shops as they also worked to build their own capacity to publish and
      circulate their ideas. Yet, there is still much to discover about those
      who produced these publications, as well as their readers. This virtual
      walk is meant to serve as a starting point for exploring the
      Spanish-language press in ways that might lead to new questions to be
      answered through additional archival exploration. Since the archive
      relevant to such exploration is fragmented and incomplete, these sites
      also serve as placeholders representing some of the aspects of the the
      thriving Spanish-language press of nineteenth-century New York City that
      have been lost to history.

In the late nineteenth century, Ann Street was lined with print shops, many of which served the neighborhood's Spanish-language press, among other communities of print.

This virtual walk explores several sites connected to the production of Spanish-language newspapers. Together these locations show that the nineteenth-century Spanish-language press in New York depended not only on writers and editors but also on printers, publishers, and booksellers who helped produce and circulate these publications. By examining the geography of these sites, we can begin to see how writers and editors including José Martí and Rafael Serra formed their intellectual community around the practical work of printing newspapers.
