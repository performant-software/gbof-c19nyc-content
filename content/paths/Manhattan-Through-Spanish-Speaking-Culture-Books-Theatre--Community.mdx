The 19th Century & Legacy of The Arts & The Press of Spanish-language Cultures in the City

In the late 19th to 20th century, Manhattan held many culture hubs that evolved through the field of the arts; bookshops, theatres and communities that served the neighborhood’s Spanish-speaking peoples and allowed for their identities to be shared throughout the city, and culture to be preserved and celebrated. 

This virtual walk explores several sites connected to the cultures and communities of the Spanish-speaking peoples. These sites also allowed for a safe space of anyone, especially those from Spain and Latin America, to all share the writings, stories, and news works amongst each other, from their cultures, and through this network and communities having been built, allows for this valuable cultural life in the 1900’s, as well; showing how immigrant communities played a major role in the landscape of the city. 

The sites are specifically connected to the arts in various forms, whether through the portrait of cultural writing at bookshops and museums, the performing arts at theatres, or through the neighborhoods that helped serve as a home for all looking for a sense of familiarity and support. A home away from home in the big city.

The performing arts, such as the theatres built shortly after the 19th century era, have proven to carry a strong legacy, bringing together the ideas, identities and voices that have first been preserved in Spanish-language print, and transforming them into shared, lived cultural experiences.
