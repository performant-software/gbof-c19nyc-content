      Behr & Kahl Booksellers was a printing, bookselling, and engraving
      business located in the current Financial District of Manhattan. It was
      operated by Charles Behr and Frederick Kahl.


      Behr & Kahl sold the poetry of Jose Maria Heredia, a Cuban exile who
      escaped to New York. The bookstore served not only as a place of business,
      but as a haven for those who could not publish their writing elsewhere.


      Heredia is just one example of a writer or activist being exiled from Cuba
      and forced to find alternate ways to distribute their work. Places like
      Behr & Kahl were crucial in their roles as Spanish-speaking printers to
      the political discourse happening in NYC. Distribution of writing allowed
      other artists and activists to find each other and create community in a
      foreign country.

      The Cuban Antislavery Committee had its headquarters and main office here.
      The group was organized by African American men who believed in the
      abolition of slavery in Cuba.


      Founding members like Samuel Raymond Scottron fought for an end to the war
      in Cuba in an effort to gain racial equality in America. They believed
      that the struggle of the enslaved Cubans was linked to their own, and that
      a war on slavery anywhere was a war on slavery everywhere. The committee
      fought for recognition by the US government in an effort to convince them
      to interfere in the conflict overseas.


      The Cuban Antislavery Committee represented an important show of support
      for Cuban exiles in New York and allowed them a space to continue their
      political education and activism from outside Cuba. Meetings consisted of
      political speeches and the circulation of Spanish-language print material,
      creating a haven of Afro-Cuban community and culture.

      La Liga de Instruccion, or The League, was an educational institution
      founded by Rafael Serra. They hosted their first official meeting on
      January 22, 1890.


      The League was created for Black Cuban and Puerto Rican men to study
      together and find community in each other. Their communities were actively
      being oppressed both in their home countries and America, and Serra felt
      it was important to teach them self-defense and learn to educate each
      other on activism and political theory.


      This is another in a line of institutions, similar to the Cuban
      Antislavery Committee, that allowed for the distribution of political
      material in Spanish and allowed Afro-Cuban men to focus on their causes in
      a supportive environment. Jose Marti was a staunch supporter of the
      League, and even believed in its ability to surpass political change and
      inspire societal change.

      Las Americas was a bookstore run by Nestor Ponce de Leon, a Cuban writer
      and activist. The store sold, printed, and bound Spanish-language
      material.


      Spaces like this were crucial in the spread of anti-slavery organization
      among both the African American and Latinx communities in NYC. Not only
      did they allow communication amongst those NYC residents, they were able
      to connect with a wide range of voices and opinions from other areas to a
      growing cultural and political movement primarily based in one city.


      Among contributions to the overall Cuban antislavery cause, Las Americas
      provided a way to make money off of the work many political activists were
      producing by selling it to the general public. Ponce de Leon's shop also
      gave journalists like Jose Marti the opportunity to publish and distribute
      Spanish-language newspapers.

      La Sociedad Literaria Hispanoamericana de Nueva York was a prominent
      aspect of the Latin American social life in NYC. Members would gather to
      hear each other speak on politics, theology, and culture. 


      This society offered a formal and dignified representation of the Latin
      American population, not just those in New York. The existence of a
      physical space in which to share ideas and organize together was a
      privilege not afforded to many Cuban exiles, or any enslaved Cubans. 


      Many founding members of the society have been linked to other sites on
      this path. Jose Marti and Nestor Ponce de Leon, to name two, are examples
      of prominent figures within Latin American and Afro-Latinx culture in NYC
      who had already published their own work and yet still believed in the
      need for a collective education within their communities. The society is
      only one of those examples.


      ![](https://core-data-tina-cms.s3.us-east-1.amazonaws.com/gbof-c19nyc/images.jpg)Retrato
      más conocido de José Martí hecho en Kingston, Jamaica (1892). Foto de Juan
      Bautista Valdés. Publicado por primera vez en Cuba en "El Fígaro" (La

      The relationship between African Americans and the Latinx population in
      NYC bred ideologies, traditions, and culture that are still alive and
      thriving today. As the founders of the Cuban Antislavery Committee
      believed, the two communities shared a struggle, both in America and in
      Cuba, and each had a responsibility to the other to fight for them. Each
      piece of journalism, literature, or theory published and sold in the
      Spanish language acted as a form of resistance. New York City in the 19th
      century was a hub for those activists and writers looking to publish
      uncensored work and live freely among their people, and this path is
      limited in that it cannot connect in any way with those still living in
      Cuba. Cubans without the means to leave or those who were enslaved had to
      rely on the efforts of others to free them, which added an urgency to the
      cause in America.

This path provides insight into how the growth of the Afro-Cuban population in NYC affected the Spanish-language press and activism scene as a whole. Cuban exiles found refuge in spaces like the Cuban Anti-Slavery Committee and La Liga de Instruccion, which allowed them to engage in political discourse and publish writing about their experiences and beliefs. This writing was then sold in bookstores owned and frequented by many of the same figures, such as Jose Marti and Nestor Ponce de Leon, who operated several shops throughout Manhattan. Each site provides evidence of a rich population with a strong cause and values in education, activism, and self-defense for Afro-Latino men in NYC. After being exiled from Cuba, several writers found community and safety in the African American community, also dealing with intense oppression and erasure in the United States. Cultural spaces, Spanish-language publishers, and booksellers offered not only an outlet for their work and a way to make money off of it, but an audience that was as committed to the cause as them and could be notified about meetings with like-minded people. A visitor would be guided through this world and engage with some of the most important locations during this time in person.
